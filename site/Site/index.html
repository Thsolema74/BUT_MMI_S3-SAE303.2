Bike Safety in France: Assessing the Impact of the Ambitious Bike Plan

The French government has, since September 14, 2018, implemented an ambitious Bike Plan with the aim of reducing the alarming number of accidents involving cyclists in the national territory. Over the past 16 years, Metropolitan France has recorded more than 70,000 bicycle accidents, a concerning situation that prompted authorities to act.

However, it is observed that Haute-Savoie, a mountainous department, has not seen a reduction in the number of accidents; on the contrary, in 2016, its figure multiplied by 8 in 2017. Narrow roads and challenges related to infrastructure have made the situation complex. 61.45% of accidents occur on departmental roads compared to 35.64% on communal roads. Although the government allocated a budget of over 300 million euros for infrastructure work, these resources were distributed among different regions of France, limiting investments in specific areas.

The question of infrastructure placement then arises. The graph reveals that most accidents occur during leisure trips, which is consistent with the tourist nature of the department. Therefore, the department had to reconcile sustainable mobility with tourist attractiveness. Indeed, on the department's website, one can find the various projects of the bike plan. Their main goal is to create cycle routes on the busiest and therefore most risky routes. "To promote and secure its practice, the Department is developing greenways and cycle routes as well as lanes or bike paths along departmental roads," explains the Haute-Savoie website.

However, should the Bike Plan be considered a total failure? The answer is nuanced. The infrastructure put in place is not in vain, and the graph above illustrates their importance eloquently. In fact, it reveals that 85% of accidents occurred in the complete absence of dedicated cyclist safety infrastructure. This means that investments in infrastructure have contributed to reducing the number of accidents for a large proportion of bicycle accidents, thus strengthening the argument in favor of the Bike Plan. Improvements to roads and specific routes clearly have a positive impact on cyclist safety. However, there is still work to be done to achieve total safety on all roads.

Regarding bicycle accidents, they affect a wide range of people, as evidenced by the statistical data presented in the box plots. The analysis of the ages of the victims highlights significant variability, illustrated by key indicators such as the standard deviation. When examining fatal accidents, there is a marked dispersion of the ages of those involved. The high standard deviation of these data, calculated at 20 years, indicates that the victims can have very different ages. Indeed, the lowest recorded age is 4 years, while the highest age reaches 85 years. This span, spanning 81 years, reflects the diversity of people affected by these fatal accidents.

In conclusion, the Bike Plan has undeniably had an impact on the frequency of bicycle accidents in France. While the positive trend is clear in most metropolitan regions, Haute-Savoie faces challenges. Is this due to mismanagement of financial resources or because Haute-Savoie is a unique place requiring customized solutions? This debate remains open, but it is essential to continue exploring ways to improve cyclist safety on our roads.
